02-15-2013 -- DoD Ch. 8-9

Chapter 8

Brooks sees a dichotomy in software design between what he terms "rationalists" and "empiricists". Provable correctness is attainable in software since just about anything you do in a program maps directly to some mathematical operation. This isn't true for most fields, and software engineers should consider themselves lucky rather than fighting with that fact. We live in the world software empiricists (I don't think empiricism is actually the right word here, more like software by heuristic) have built, and it's buggy and crashes all the time. I think his argument about software proofs being unworkable for anything bigger than a kernel assumes you write a proof about the whole thing at once. Software should be composed of smaller, simple, provably correct pieces. This isn't always possible, but it's not a waste of time either: see the re-emergence of functional programming as concurrent programs become very difficult to reason about.

Chapter 9

Chapter 9 has a pretty simple message: Don't assume everyone is on the same page about design goals. Things shouldn't be implicit, they should be explicit. Even an attempt to make them explicit can even be useful to the design as it sometimes exposes ignorance and faulty assumptions. Moreover, at least everyone is working toward the same goal, correct or not.

02-08-2013 -- DoD Ch. 6-7

Chapter 6

Chapter 6 is about the move from design by individual to design by teams. He suggests that most great "design" has previously been the product of individuals. He claims that we do team design now primarily to deal with increased complexity and tighter schedules. The challenges this team design bring are around division of labor and keeping the group of collaborators in sync. Everyone must share the same knowledge (training, materials, etc.) to work on the design. He goes on at length about the idea of "conceptual integrity". While collaboration on a design does have some benefits, Brooks thinks that a single person must, in the end, be in charge. His job would be to maintain this "conceptual integrity". He also talks about design reviews, where the team of collaborators meet and and share their work with each other. Brooks is high on two man teams, probably the source of all the "pair programming" methodologies that are popular right now.

Chapter 7

Chapter 7 is about collaboration by workers/designers who aren't in the same room. This is a strange topic to cover in a book since the limits here are mostly technological and change rapidly. There's some talk about how important interfaces/apis/encapsulation are. This is all certainly true, and by now well accepted. Overall, this chapter felt dated despite the 2010 publication date. The videoconferencing tools are much better these days, and some of the limitations he brings up are only a problem if you don't gear your meeting specifically to that medium. I personally think face to face interaction may be overvalued.

02-02-2013 --DoD Ch. 4-5

Chapter 4

Chapter four is largely about the requirements part of the design process. Brooks points out problems with the typical process of laying out requirement: that there are usually too many of them and that they aren't prioritized which makes decision making by engineers more difficult. If there are no weightings on requirements and the constraints don't allow for all of them to be satisfied, how do you decide which to satisfy and which to ignore? There should be fewer, more general requirements and there should be some individual who makes the ultimate decisions on them. Unfortunately, the need for work to be done by contract complicates this, and there's no perfect solution despite the suggestion to contract the design and implementation seperately.

Chapter 5

Chapter 5 is about replacing the waterfall model. First Brooks argues that there needs to be a single, teachable model to replace the waterfall. He likes the spiral model, but would like for the interaction between designer and user to be more formally specified as a series of points on the spiral where contracts can be made. He talks a while about open source as a design model, but that is of clearly limited application. You can't open source an electron microscope since building them and using them are two different skillsets and two different groups of people.

01-27-2013 -- DoD Ch. 1-3

Chapter 1

In chapter one, Brooks frames the book by giving a few definitions and setting out the major questions he's trying to answer. Design is, informally, the plan of action or "mental formulation" of some thing that needs to be created or implemented. The "design concept" is a little vague, but appears to be a set of very high level desiderata; maybe things like "simplicity." This seems to of use primarily as an organizing principle for communication between designers. The book is primarily about the generality of design approaches across different disciplines, and is focused on what he calls "original design" as opposed to iterations or modifications of existing designs. I'm not sure how much actual work gets done from scratch like that.

Chapter 2

Chapter two is mostly about "The Rational Model," or formal approaches based on things like decision trees or the waterfall approach. These are attempts to provide direction to the design process. As such they are useful, but attempting to use them as a recipe is probably a mistake. The waterfall is too rigid and the problem space is usually too large to tackle with a decision tree.

Chapter 3

Clearly the models from chapter 2 don't really work very well in practice. One problem is the need for formal requirements up front. This is rarely available. Additionally, you can never be sure of the impact of any given design decision until after you've finished. Decisions are inter-related, and those relationships are not always well defined. Constraints can also change during the process. A better approach would take into account the iterative nature of design. There probably isn't an approach that would make everyone happy, but even a flawed system might prove useful.
