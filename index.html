01-27-2013 -- DoD Ch. 1-3

Chapter 1

In chapter one, Brooks frames the book by giving a few definitions and setting out the major questions he's trying to answer. Design is, informally, the plan of action or "mental formulation" of some thing that needs to be created or implemented. The "design concept" is a little vague, but appears to be a set of very high level desiderata; maybe things like "simplicity." This seems to of use primarily as an organizing principle for communication between designers. The book is primarily about the generality of design approaches across different disciplines, and is focused on what he calls "original design" as opposed to iterations or modifications of existing designs. I'm not sure how much actual work gets done from scratch like that.

Chapter 2

Chapter two is mostly about "The Rational Model," or formal approaches based on things like decision trees or the waterfall approach. These are attempts to provide direction to the design process. As such they are useful, but attempting to use them as a recipe is probably a mistake. The waterfall is too rigid and the problem space is usually too large to tackle with a decision tree.

Chapter 3

Clearly the models from chapter 2 don't really work very well in practice. One problem is the need for formal requirements up front. This is rarely available. Additionally, you can never be sure of the impact of any given design decision until after you've finished. Decisions are inter-related, and those relationships are not always well defined. Constraints can also change during the process. A better approach would take into account the iterative nature of design. There probably isn't an approach that would make everyone happy, but even a flawed system might prove useful.
